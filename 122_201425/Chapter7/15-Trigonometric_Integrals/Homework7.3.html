  9. Consider the trigonmetric identity \(\sin{(A+B)}=\sin{A}\cos{B}+\cos{A}\sin{B}\)

    1. Use this identity to derive an identity for \(\sin{(A-B)}\) in terms of \(\sin{A}\), \(\cos{A}\), \(\sin{B}\), and \(\cos{B}\).

      \(\sin{(A-B)}=\sin{A}\cos{B}-\cos{A}\sin{B}\)

    2. Use the given identity and your answer for part (a) to derive the following identity: \[\sin{A}\cos{B}=\frac{1}{2}\left[\sin{(A-B)}+\sin{(A+B)}\right]\]

      1Adding the given identity to the identity from part (a) and then dividing both sides by 2 yields the desired result.

  10. Consider the trigonmetric identity \(\cos{(A+B)}=\cos{A}\cos{B}-\sin{A}\sin{B}\)

    1. Use this identity to derive an identity for \(\cos{(A-B)}\) in terms of \(\sin{A}\), \(\cos{A}\), \(\sin{B}\), and \(\cos{B}\).

      \(\cos{(A-B)}=\cos{A}\cos{B}+\sin{A}\sin{B}\)

    2. Use the given identity and your answer for part (a) to derive the following identity: \[\cos{A}\cos{B}=\frac{1}{2}\left[\cos{(A-B)}+\cos{(A+B)}\right]\]

      1Adding the given identity to the identity from part (a) and then dividing both sides by 2 yields the desired result.

    3. Use the given identity and your answer for part (a) to derive the following identity: \[\sin{A}\sin{B}=\frac{1}{2}\left[\cos{(A-B)}-\cos{(A+B)}\right]\]

      1Subtracting the given identity from the identity from part (a) and then dividing both sides by 2 yields the desired result.

  8. Consider \(\int_{\pi/2}^\pi \sec{x} \,dx\)

    1. Explain why this integral is improper.

      1The integral is improper because \(\sec{x}\) has an infinite discontinuity at \(x=\frac{\pi}{2}\) which is the lower limit of integration.

    2. Evaluate the given integral. If it diverges, explain why.

      The integral diverges because \(\int_{\pi/2}^{\pi} \sec{x} \,dx = -\infty\)

    1. Use integration by parts to evaluate \(\int \sec^{3}{(x)}\,dx\). (Hint: \(\sec^3{x}=\sec^2{x}\sec{x}\) and \(\tan^2{x}=\sec^2{x}-1\))
